About Neurosurgery

What is Neurosurgery

Neurosurgery is a medical specialty that provides comprehensive care for patients with complex neurological conditions. Neurosurgery not only diagnoses and treats disorders of the brain, spinal cord, and peripheral nerves, but also performs a variety of interventions. Neurosurgeons use advanced techniques to remove brain tumours, repair spinal injuries, and treat other conditions that affect the nervous system. Neurosurgery also has a significant impact beyond the operating room, as it develops treatments for chronic pain, epilepsy, movement disorders, and psychiatric conditions, improving the quality of life for millions of people worldwide.

Common Neurology Conditions

Stroke

Stroke occurs when blood flow to a part of the brain is interrupted or reduced, causing damage to brain cells. This condition can result from a blocked blood vessel or bleeding in the brain, and if not treated promptly, may lead to permanent disability or death.

Treatment services include

early detection, rapid assessment, medical and advanced treatments, rehabilitation, and preventive measures to reduce the risk of recurrent stroke.

Read More

Brain tumours

Brain tumours are abnormal growths of cells in the brain or spinal canal. They can be benign or malignant and may develop in the brain itself or spread from other parts of the body .

Treatment services include

early detection through advanced imaging, surgery and medical therapies, advanced treatments such as Gamma Knife and awake craniotomy, and ongoing care to monitor recurrence, manage side effects, and improve quality of life.

Read More

Alzheimer’s Disease

Alzheimer’s Disease is the most common type of dementia, causing gradual decline in memory, thinking, and behaviour due to changes in the brain. Early diagnosis and continuous care can help manage symptoms and improve quality of life.

Care services include:

early detection, cognitive assessments, advanced imaging, supportive treatments, rehabilitation, and ongoing monitoring to slow progression and provide better patient care.
